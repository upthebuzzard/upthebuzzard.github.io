# Eclectic Blog Content Review

**Date**: 2026-04-03
**Participants**: Claude (summary & assessment), Gemini 2.5 Pro (feedback)

---

## Content Summary

The site has 11 collections (~83 posts) grouped into 4 zones under a single author (Chris Gathercole, 25+ years in tech). The tagline is "A Smattering of Stories" but the content goes well beyond stories.

### Zone A: Stories (homepage-featured core)

- **Moose & Goose** (11 posts, 2011) — Children's bedtime detective series, ages 4-11. Interactive pauses where listeners contribute ideas. Problems solved through lateral thinking. E.g. training seagulls to poop suncream; keeping custard warm in water beds.
- **Grey Parrot** (2 posts, 2011) — Shorter children's stories, ages 3-6. A wise-but-obtuse parrot gives technically correct but maddeningly convoluted answers. Riddle/puzzle structure.
- **Predicting the Present** (21 posts, 2012-2022) — Flash fiction extrapolating current trends (Doctorow/Sterling style). Satirical, thought-provoking. Many written for 48-96 hour challenges.
- **Emus All The Way Down** (14 posts, 2013-2018) — Serialised speculative novel spun off from Predicting the Present. Douglas Adams-esque. Slow publication, uncertain destination.
- **Fragments** (10 posts, 2017-2025) — Short standalone pieces. Observations, memoir, experimental writing. Introspective and meta.
- **Haiku** (2 posts, 2019) — Experimental poetry beyond 5-7-5.

### Zone B: Pretensions (separate professional identity)

- **Pretensions** (4 posts, 2017 + 2024-2025) — Professional articles on product, tech, strategy. Some imported from LinkedIn. Authoritative but accessible. Recently revived.

### Zone C: Hobbies (specialist interests)

- **Games** (3 posts, 2017) — Card game design notes. Iterative design documented from 200-word challenge to full rulebook.
- **Hifi** (4 posts, 2023) — Affordable audio equipment essays. Memoir of bad purchases turned lockdown research project.

### Zone D: Deprecated (live but hidden)

- **Overgeneralisations** (2 posts, 2019) — Opinion pieces on social networks. Superseded by Pretensions.
- **Jekyll Notes** (10 posts, 2017) — Technical reference for building the site.
- **Distractions** (0 posts) — Curated links, dormant.

### Cross-cutting themes

1. **Problem-solving as narrative drive** — from children's detective stories to business strategy
2. **Technology and society** — speculative fiction extrapolates trends; professional articles critique impact
3. **Playfulness in serious contexts** — strategy articles use Ted Lasso analogies; children's stories teach through humour
4. **Transparency and iteration** — admits uncertainty, documents learning, invites feedback
5. **Observation as material** — mining everyday life for insight and story

### The central tension

The site's identity is "A Smattering of Stories" but it contains professional strategy articles, game design rulebooks, hifi reviews, technical documentation, and curated links. The zone system manages this with separate branding/navigation per zone, but the question is whether this eclecticism works for readers or fragments the audience.

## Gemini Feedback

### How it works as an eclectic blog

**Strengths**: Authenticity (represents a whole person), creative cross-pollination (the same problem-solving mind across domains), serendipity (a reader arriving for strategy might discover the fiction).

**Risks**: Audience fragmentation (a parent subscribing for Moose & Goose gets product strategy updates), diluted SEO discoverability (hard for search engines to categorise), cognitive dissonance (the tagline sets expectations the content exceeds).

**Verdict**: The breadth currently weakens the site more than it strengthens it, primarily because the structure fights the diversity rather than embracing and explaining it. The opportunity lies in reframing eclecticism as a core feature, not a bug.

### Audience management and the zone system

The zone system works as internal signposting for readers already on the site, but doesn't solve the "front door" problem — how does a new visitor know which zone is for them? It creates sub-identities sharing a domain rather than a unified authorial identity.

**Three alternative models proposed:**

1. **Hub and Spokes** — Stories as the hub (main identity), other zones as spokes. Homepage features stories prominently; other content presented as side projects. Risk: devalues non-story content; locks in an identity that may not match the author's evolving interests.

2. **Author as Brand** — Shift focus from content types to the author. Unifying principle is "the work of Chris Gathercole" rather than "stories." Tagline changes to something like "Curious explorations in story, tech, and strategy." Risk: lives or dies on the strength of the author's biography; can feel self-indulgent.

3. **Separate Identities** (subdomains) — Distinct homes for distinct audiences. Risk: increases maintenance, splits SEO, severs serendipitous discovery. Most drastic option.

### Content identity

The current tagline "A Smattering of Stories" is charming but now misleading. It undersells the professional/analytical content and creates expectation mismatch. The core question: **Is this a website about stories that happens to contain other things, or a website about a person who happens to write stories, articles, and notes?**

### What's most distinctive

1. **Moose & Goose Stories** — The combination of specific age target, detective format, interactive pauses, and lateral thinking focus is highly original. The site's most unique "product."

2. **The intersection of tech & fiction** — Predicting the Present + Emus + Pretensions together create a powerful meta-theme: a technology practitioner creatively and critically examining its human impact. This synthesis is the author's unique selling proposition.

**Recommendation**: Lean into this intersection. The author is not just a storyteller *and* a tech professional; they are a storyteller *because* they are a tech professional. Making this connection explicit would provide coherence.

### What's missing or underserved

1. **An "Operating Thesis" / "Start Here" page** — Acknowledge the eclecticism, explain the philosophy, guide different readers to relevant zones. Could retroactively unify the entire site.

2. **Connecting the dots** — Essays or index pages linking disparate content around shared themes. E.g. "Playful Systems" linking a Moose & Goose story, the Greedy Devils game design, and the Ted Lasso strategy article.

3. **The author's voice on the eclecticism** — A meta blog post reflecting on the challenge of managing this mix. Would name the tension and invite the audience into the process.

4. **Audio format** — The interactive Moose & Goose stories are practically begging for podcast/audio adaptation. The radio play in Predicting the Present shows the author has the skills.

## Assessment of Feedback

Gemini's analysis is sharp and well-targeted. Key takeaways:

**The diagnosis is sound.** The site's eclecticism is genuinely both its greatest asset and its biggest challenge. The observation that the structure currently *fights* the diversity rather than embracing it rings true — the zone system manages the problem technically but doesn't solve it editorially.

**The "Author as Brand" model is the most interesting suggestion** for this site specifically. The author's cross-domain problem-solving perspective is genuinely distinctive, and the existing transparency/meta-awareness in the writing style would support it well. However, it requires confidence in the author's personal brand being compelling enough to unify the content — which depends on how the author wants to present themselves.

**The "Start Here" page idea is the highest-impact, lowest-cost suggestion.** A single page that names the eclecticism, explains the philosophy, and guides readers could dramatically improve the first-visit experience. It doesn't require restructuring anything — just adding one page.

**The "connecting the dots" suggestion is compelling but labour-intensive.** Cross-linking essays around shared themes would make the eclecticism feel intentional rather than accidental, but it's a significant ongoing editorial commitment.

**The audio suggestion for Moose & Goose is spot-on** but represents a major format expansion rather than a site architecture decision.

**Where the feedback is weaker:** The SEO concern is valid in theory but may not matter much for a personal blog that isn't monetised. The "separate subdomains" suggestion is correctly identified as the worst option. The tagline critique assumes the tagline needs to describe all content rather than just set a tone — "A Smattering of Stories" could work if the homepage makes the broader scope clear.

**Bottom line:** The most actionable insight is that the site needs to *explain itself* — not restructure itself. A "Start Here" page and some cross-linking would go further than any architectural change.
