tl;dr   First identify a strategically relevant portfolio of versatile product capabilities, and only then prioritise the pieces.

Increasingly these days, organisations take a product approach, and with good reason. The emphasis on a customer-centric view, validating, meeting, and tracking changing customer needs, creates user features that are part of a coherent, effective whole. This is supported by copious literature and shared best practices.

But when paired with a per-product (or per-feature) prioritisation process, there are insidious downsides.

In prioritising only for an individual product, and not across all likely products, you inevitably end up making local decisions that harm the organisation as a whole in the longer term. There is a marked reluctance to commit to large, shared capabilities, or even to acknowledge some products are better considered as capabilities for multiple products. The classic escalation is to double down on the next Product discovery and prioritisation effort, to maximise the benefits while avoiding the costs, not addressing these consequences. To ‘Product’ harder, in other words. And the negatives worsen.

Shocking claims, if true.

Enter Capability-First thinking, a subtle shift in mindset that first identifies a strategically relevant portfolio of versatile product capabilities, and only in that context considers how to prioritise individual products and efficiently sequence development of pieces of capabilities. This approach enhances an organisation’s ability to rapidly innovate, reduces costs of subsequent products and idea validation, and creates a solid foundation for sustainable, long-term growth.

At its core, Capability-First thinking recognises that product capabilities — the skills, processes, and technologies that enable an organisation to deliver value via products — are more enduring and versatile, more valuable, than any individual product. It bridges the gap between strategy and execution, providing a shared vocabulary for both Product and Tech functions to collaborate and plan effectively. The Product Mindset is still important, but it can be used in a better way.

> There’s a scene in Ted Lasso (the streaming series with the highest density of character arcs, evs. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ted_Lasso)), where Jamie “Prick” Tartt, the star player, explains to the dressing room (3–0 down at half time), how to make better use of him: “Don’t play to me. Play through me… That’s Total Football”. The team ‘get’ it, and return to the pitch for the second half, whereupon they score a “bloody gorgeous” goal with the ball pinging back and forth via Jamie before he lays it off for a teammate to score

As a side-effect, Capability-First thinking provides a tangible overview of where an org would ‘like’ to go (based on the collective hopes and dreams). This can be contrasted with where it is going in practice (based on what products are enabled by the actual, in-play capabilities, and the existing prioritisation process). We can easily construct a framework for a practical strategy to head in an agreed direction, based on a defined portfolio of capabilities.

Clearly, too good to be true. Or perhaps, too obvious not to have been thought of multiple times before now, presumably with better branding. The weaknesses inherent in product-first and per-product prioritisation are so serious and blatant, surely they have not been overlooked?

## The Insidious Downsides of Product-First combined with per-Product Prioritisation

Amidst the flurry of mid-project decisions that always crop up, it is easy, even likely, to lose sight of the bigger picture. With no agreed context, each local compromise risks leading to significant, longer term harm.

This is almost a textbook definition of tech debt, and it will inexorably increase.

Cross-product synergies will be overlooked, since they are expressly not how each product is incentivised. “_This_ product is all about a laser-like focus.”

It shies away from big technical commitments, biasing efforts towards short term hacks.

If the Tech function wants to plan for the longer term, it leaves them with just guesswork and a damage limitation exercise. You have pitted Product against Tech, an unhealthy conflict, where each group has very different priorities.

Nimbleness is lost. Useful functionality is locked up in individual products, unavailable outside of the ‘owning’ team to the rest of the org for subsequent quick experiments.

There is no incentive to improve things in a general sense that can withstand each selfishly-prioritised product in turn. The term ‘selfish’ is chosen to describe each set of  project folk acting as they should according to their incentives.

Growth is unsustainable. Each product has its own additional resource demands. You will ‘use up’ all your teams very quickly, inefficiently allocated to individual products, and then … no more new products.

## How to think Capability-First

- Accumulate the superset of everything any part of your org is or might be interested in now or in the future

- Distil A Portfolio Of Capabilities

- Prioritise and Sequence Products Around the Capability Pieces

- Mid-Project Attitude

### Accumulate the superset of everything any part of your org is or might be interested in

Let’s address the goldmine, the treasure trove that is all those hopes and dreams the people in an org have, the half-baked product ideas, info and understanding about customers, their pain points and feedback, the full lists of prioritised and lowly or not-yet-even prioritised projects, every utterance by a HIPPO ([explanation](https://www.uservoice.com/blog/highest-paid-persons-opinion)), the kinds of things likely to be considered soon.

The superset of what the entire org is or might be interested in.

All of it. Quantity most definitely beats quality.

Thousands of line items? Bring it on. No filtering.

This is the good stuff. You had it all along.

NB, it is worth ensuring you have a naming or tagging convention in place so, later, when the analysis is done, you can associate every idea with who or where it came from.

### Distil a Portfolio Of Capabilities

For the next few days, a small coterie of analytically- and architecturally-minded Product and Tech folk run a paper shuffling exercise (or use spreadsheets, YMMV).

All the ideas are distilled down, grouped into categories by similarity, by common hypothetical capabilities, distilled, re-grouped, down and down, with different levels of abstraction, until we start settling on a core set of emergent capabilities. Rinse and repeat.

Try again with a different mindset when thinking about the capabilities an idea might need.

1000s of ideas

→ 100s of groups

←→ 10s of deeper categories

←→ 10s of deeper still categories …

←→ 10s of capabilities

Try again with a different mindset when thinking about the capabilities an idea might need.

This is actually a very simple, iterative process. Grouping items by theme is a common ritual, as is grouping items to uncover emergent, shared themes. We are good at it. It is easy and quick. There is no rocket science needed, rather a nuanced sense of how product ideas relate to each other and customers, and an intuition around what common pieces of functionality, data, or logic can be most effective in achieving them.

Each capability is justified by the subset of ideas that demanded it.

We can now reclassify every idea by which subset of capabilities each would require.

- Are some ideas just not being catered for?

- Are certain capabilities standing out as core to almost everything, and others as particularly high cost for only minor payoffs?

- Are certain subsets of capabilities punching above their weight, supporting a high proportion of strong candidate ideas?

- Which parts of what capabilities would enable us to validate the most product ideas the soonest?

- ...

If this step has been effective, your newly defined portfolio of capabilities will now be suggesting suites of new, eminently doable ideas.

### Examples of possible capabilities

A brief excursion into examples of possible capabilities, obvs biased by many great years spent in a news org:

- The absolute classic, slam dunk, hands down winner of Most Capable of Capabilities:™: of any org that has lots of text content is… a generic text+metadata search API of all its content. It would support any and all product ideas that involve slicing and dicing your content. Without it, you are thoroughly hobbled. Don’t confuse it with the product feature ‘site search’.

- Followableness — or, supporting the ability for users to lock onto an item of interest, such as a topic, keyword, author, holiday destination, paint colours, etc, and be alerted when anything new happens to it. Considered as a Product feature this would be “follow a topic”, but considered as a capability this is “follow almost any type of thing, even things we have not thought of yet, but let’s start with topics”.

- Self-serve user accounts. A big, complicated challenge.

- Auto-summarise text using a particular ‘voice’ — very contemporary, this. Wrap the LLM (the raw ‘possibility’) in guardrails, and open it up for safe, sensible, reliable flexible use, turning it into a capability.

- The ability to think of, validate, design, develop, deploy small ideas, quickly is itself a capability that requires care and effort. Simply demanding it is not sufficient. If this aspect crops up as being significant, commit to it as an explicit capability.

- ….

NB, these are not simply instances of useful low level tech, like a database or search engine. These are tailored, data rich, complex, product capabilities, working systems but not quite full products in their own right. Perhaps a better way to think of them is shareable, transferable, reusable, generic product features, requiring only the lightest of config and UI skins to make them relevant and specific to a new product.

### Whew. A defined portfolio of capabilities, with solid, agreed justifications and ambitions.

The makings of our strategy are right there, as is an opportunity to assess how well aligned our existing capabilities are with what has emerged from this analysis. Ammunition for a reorg. We cover these aspects below.

We have a direction of travel for the org, containing strong hints for the future. Tech can start thinking about and working on the longer-term capabilities. Do we need to develop new skill sets, to understand new tech, to clear the deck for what is to come?

More to the point, everyone agrees on this being the approach. This is how we are going to develop all those products and gain customer learnings most efficiently and over the long haul. These are the things we are going to get better and better at doing. The portfolio of capabilities is the explicit, dare we say strategic, bet we are making.

### Prioritise and Sequence Products Around the Capability Pieces

If anyone feels left out, perhaps reluctant to join at first but now in full FOMO having seen how well the distillation process went, its a perfect opportunity to sense check the output. Assess the previously overlooked ideas against the Capability Portfolio. Ideally, they should just slot right in (but might be worth tweaking things just a bit so they know they were listened to…).

We have a prioritisation choice now:

- Validate the most the soonest?

- Head straight for the strongest looking product idea (or set thereof),

- Pick the most interesting subset of capabilities that lead to the quickest validation and the biggest likely payoff?

- Look for a compromise of the most promising idea that is closest to being achievable, given the capabilities in play?

- …

Work backwards to identify and sequence the development of the minimum pieces of each capability we need to develop in order to tackle the chosen product idea, and the level of performance needed at this stage.

Tech and Product are in harmony now, each trying to give the other what they need, namely:

- A shared vocabulary. By all means call these Product Capabilities.

- An agreed approach. We don’t have to keep re-arguing the main principles.

- The opportunity to build marvellous, potent products that reach the customer parts that other products just couldn’t.

- Enough lead time and structure to build good capabilities, in such a way as to be most useful asap.

We make a single decision, to build a specific product or feature but now in the context of the portfolio of capabilities, and the sequencing of the pieces of those capabilities. The full context flows out to the respective team(s) tasked with implementation.

### Mid-Project Attitude

As ever, pragmatic, painful, local product and tech decisions will need to be made mid-project, but this time they are properly informed. They have been given an explicit context. The consequences can be accurately understood in that context. Some shortcuts will be ‘bad’, harmful to the bigger picture, some fine, some fine for now. The consequences for the sequencing of all capability pieces is known.

In a very real sense, strategic-level understanding has been carried all the way through to the project practitioners making in-the-moment decisions.

For those capability pieces constructed as part of this project, some effort goes into understanding the more general use cases, and informs the initial design. It need be nothing more onerous than a simple chat with likely future clients of the capability.

Data is well structured and clean, and honours best data practice, from the start. No excuses.

We’ve learned something new about our customers that is not directly relevant to this project! Add it to the shared mix of customer info, alert the relevant teams. Don’t discard it or ignore it.

Of course (Bezos API mandate, [blog](https://nordicapis.com/the-bezos-api-mandate-amazons-manifesto-for-externalization/)[)](https://nordicapis.com/the-bezos-api-mandate-amazons-manifesto-for-externalization/)) the capability pieces are exposed to the rest of the org right from the start (not later, when or if convenient), via simple, documented, discoverable, versioned, tested, monitored, authenticated, self-serve APIs. There is an assumption of good behaviour by other teams, or actual read/write limits baked in if trust is lacking.

The above guard rails around APIs would be an example of a discovered, high priority capability: namely the ability to rapidly, easily, and repeatedly spin up a new internal API. Would be daft if every team had to start from scratch each time.

Were another team wanting to add significant load or data volume to the APIs, or in need of a delayed capability piece, they are welcome to contribute to the work needed to strengthen and improve the APIs.

A product is as thin a skin as possible (and sensible) over a composition of potent, generic product capabilities.

It is a warning sign when a product requires significant bespoke effort that is not contributing towards a shared capability. The question has to be asked: why?

Whereas, pro-active “aha” moments that advance the state of the portfolio of capabilities are always welcome.

The project is all about the product feature and the capabilities. It is assessed on the delivery of both.

A project or product does not ‘own’ a capability. It is merely an early steward of it.

## Capability-First as Strategy

This deserves an entire article in its own right.

From the discussion of what constitutes a good strategy ([on this site](/pretensions/2024-06-14-so-you-think-you-have-a-strategy.html)), we have these primary aspects:

- Diagnosis: simplifies complex reality, and identifies critical aspects/risks/opportunities.

- Guiding Policy: overall approach, to cope with or overcome the obstacles/opportunities identified in the diagnosis.

- Coherent Actions: coordinated, defined steps, to carry out the guiding policy.

For a good Diagnosis you need “a correct understanding of what is going on outside the org, as well as what is going on inside the org.”, aka, “good situational awareness”. The analysis that goes into distilling the initial Portfolio Of Capabilities provides just that, tapping into viewpoints from across the org. You now have a direction of travel. Are you happy with it?

The Guiding Policy is to create and maintain a clearly defined, up to date Portfolio of Capabilities, and prioritise around it. Basically, go all in on Capability-First. This is primarily how you minimise the likelihood of “perverse incentives”. This Guiding Policy is clear, meaningful, practical, and relevant to all.

The Coherent Actions are

- Starting with the initial distillation, create a process to regularly review the Portfolio Of Capabilities. Is it drifting? Is it still relevant? Do the capabilities make practical sense? Have we thought of a way to improve the portfolio?

- Prioritise and sequence projects around the Capability Pieces.

- Review existing business processes and team hierarchies. Identify the ones which resist or obstruct. You might want to ponder a reorg to support the Portfolio Of Capabilities.

- If a product is proposed that is at odds with the Portfolio Of Capabilities, do we need to rethink our portfolio? Maybe so, but now it is explicitly a strategic-level consideration, possibly an actual change in strategy, and not disguised as a ‘simple’ product prioritisation decision.

- Assess all project choices and successes in the context of Portfolio Of Capabilities

And that is it: a practical strategy.

## Ah, yes, but, no, but

**1.** Critics might argue that Capability-First thinking could lead to over-engineering or wasted efforts.

YAGNI is a much abused concept, a mantra lazily applied in development. Only build what you need, when you need it. ([wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/You_aren%27t_gonna_need_it)). When applied carelessly, or without an awareness of the wider context in which a system sits, YAGNI is akin to committing the deeper tech sin of “premature optimisation” ([wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Program_optimization#When_to_optimize?trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block)), where you make short term code/architecture decisions, for implicit, undefined reasons (which can include impatience, laziness, or a laser-like focus on this product), and they turn out to be a hindrance later.

If implemented thoughtfully, Capability-First actually reduces waste by ensuring that every development effort contributes to the organisation’s overall capability set. It addresses core causes of technical debt and siloed thinking by encouraging the creation of well-structured, shareable capabilities from the outset.

**2.** All those half-baked ideas! How can they possibly be considered seriously? They have not yet been validated!

The ensemble of ideas is the thing. You have sampled a big space of possibilities. Statistics is on your side. It helps identify possible potent capabilities, signified by clusters of related product possibilities. It helps you target validation efforts.

You can now reassess your favoured candidate products in the light of the portfolio of capabilities. Do they still look as worthwhile? You certainly have a better sense of how to achieve them.

Compare that to the likelihood of the one, top priority, ‘discovered’, bet-the-farm Product being the single best thing your org could be doing, to the detriment of everything else.

**3.** Surely the Portfolio of Capabilities will be a shifting target, arbitrary in its specific details? Try it again next week, with new product ideas added, and you end up with different capabilities. How can you plan long term around that?

So far, this has not been the way it goes. As mentioned earlier, “Rinse and repeat. Try a different mindset” is a key aspect of the distillation process. The distilled set of capabilities is quite stable. A good test of this stability is to work through the consequences of adding new, previously unseen product ideas.

If several different, viable portfolios emerge. Pick one. There will be some reason why one is better suited to your org than the other. Perhaps it is more compatible with your existing team structures?

**4.** Project teams always have tough, pragmatic, mid-project decisions to make. Each time, some aspect, some initiative has to lose. Why complain? It’s the way of the world.

Capability-First offers a reframing that clarifies what is actually at stake in each argument:

- Concrete, explicit, high priority, short-term product necessities, now now now

vs

- Previously implicit, wishy washy, strategic-adjacent, long term, and almost by definition low priority, lets worry about it later.

The pressures accumulate in the routinely overlooked low priority, for the greater good, choices, until boom, that hole we’ve been digging for ourselves becomes a crisis. The “in the moment” team will often lack the context to make the best decision each time.

A well chosen capability will be, by definition, of likely and immediate use to other teams. A decision to harm or delay it needs careful consideration. At least now you can see that is the case.

**5.** This is just a rehash of a Platform-based approach. A Tech thing.

Nope. Sure, you might decide that capabilities better live in a ‘platform’, with dedicated platform teams, or a virtual platform, with every team behaving in an agreed, platform-y way, or simply that the first team which needs a particular capability is doomed to support it.

But the main point is everyone deciding and agreeing on what the capabilities are, incorporating them into the specs of the prioritised projects, and treating them like the strategic pieces that they are.

We know that many microservices come with complexity baggage, and the rule for microservices is generally DO repeat yourself, breaking years of engineering doctrine. But monoliths have their own interdependency baggage too."

[Mark Ridley](https://uk.linkedin.com/in/markridley)
    (added 2024-08-28)

Aha. This is a topic of particular interest (and pain) to me.

If your org has significant data and/or content, whether generated internally or imported, for use internally or to meet external customers' needs, but does not have “a generic text+metadata search API of all its content” then this is a no-brainer: make one. It is likely to be the single most useful capability you could add. I have experienced making one, having one, having one die a slow lingering death without replacement, being told we have one when in practice we don’t, teams belatedly realising how much more empowered they are (with one), nearly having one but it is tied to a specific product and so is not shared, and much more more.

A recent twist on “a generic text+metadata search API of all its content” is that it should now also support vector search. See the discussion about “This bit of text is like that bit of text” ([article](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/mind-ai-gap-chris-gathercole-vd1we)).

As for moving too slowly, yes, this can be an issue if the search capability is not recognised as a strategic central search capability and prioritised as such. “Moving too slowly” can include: not fixing (or even looking for) data quality issues, not adding new data fields, not supporting likely increased load, not accepting supplemental dev assistance from teams who have a specific aspect they want to add or enhance, not accepting change requests from outside of the ‘owning’ product, getting in a muddle with data versioning, getting stuck on old, unsupported tech, not being able to handle new categories, being held hostage by poor data quality from other parts of the org, not having clear guiding principles, etc.

If an autonomous team really does want MOAR!!!, it is still in a better position if there is a strategic central search capability in place. Initial R&D can happen straight away. Done well, the constituent pieces of the search capability would be available, through APIs (content feeds, taxonomies, etc), and the autonomous team can go hard core and build their own instance, adding their specific quirks, with minimal overhead.

That said, my experience has been that if you have pressure building from autonomous teams wanting to do their own thing, the critical problem is with the allegedly "strategic central" aspect of the search capability. I would suggest that perhaps it isn’t (strategic and central), or was not considered as such early enough. The fact that teams might want to build their own is a symptom of a deeper problem.

Ah, “budget considerations”. What an impactful, consequence-ridden phrase. I feel your pain.

There has to be an explicit “budget considerations” equivalent of Conway’s Law ([wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway%27s_law)), given that “budget considerations” will be one of the main drivers for org structure and reporting lines.

Many massively frustrating, fruitless conversations were had, standing over the smashed pieces of hopes and dreams and common sense, with the phrase “but that’s not how we are funded” still echoing in the air.

7.1 "In my experience products and the MVP approval (which becomes a perfect vehicle for scope creep) work as line items in an annual budget."

This is basically the main point that I am making: serial prioritisation of products is bad. You end up achieving less. It is blinkered, short term-ist, and a refusal to accept the responsibilities of crafting a decent strategy.

7.2 "How might you fit budgeting for capabilities? My initial approach is to imagine an earmark for “meta projects” which are just the sort of thing you’re thinking of. Get each product team to pay a meta tax out of their budget to support key capabilities that will serve multiple products and improve user experience which of course is my focus."

I would offer the following thoughts:

- The simple act of going through the capability distillation process up front, a few days of effort, done once, considering 100s or 1000s of possible projects, creates a resource and a mindset that can be used to improve subsequent, serially-prioritised projects.

- At some stage, it has to become clear that if serially-prioritised Projects A, C, and F, all required a common capability, and the capability was rebuilt each time, for some repeated cost, there is a solid, cost-based case to be made that perhaps it is worth working out a way to persist that capability.

- Avoid the term “tax”. The capability is a measurable opportunity for saving time and costs. Rejoice! The “tax” comes from repeatedly and needlessly rebuilding the same capability.

- Capabilities can be virtual, taking the form of ever-improving boilerplate code and systems. The project can still be assessed on its meeting the headline goal and improved boilerplate code for subsequent projects.

7.3 "The problem I’m trying to solve is avoiding compromise on user experience to stay within budget because tech costs are overspent before the UI is finalized, or nice to haves get finished. Improved search across all products is one victim of this"

This classic fear of Tech “boiling the ocean” when all Product wanted was a small UI change is both a valid consideration and completely avoidable.

You should only build what is needed for the current project, but do it in such a way that you are mindful of the bigger picture. When taking shortcuts, some will matter long term and won’t. The capabilities mindset just gives a clearer view in those moments.

There is a good analogy with TDD (Test Driven Development). In almost every dev project, you move seamlessly from “it’s so small and short term that writing tests will just delay me” to “I wish I had been writing tests right from the start because now it’s a bit of a mess and retro-fitting tests will be a pain”. Good, sensible, pragmatic TDD can easily lead to reduced dev time, and a higher quality implementation. What’s not to like?

Having the capabilities portfolio explicit from the start would help with in-the-moment architectural decisions, in much the same way.

7.4 "Keen to hear any thoughts on that / experience you have on budgeting capabilities."

The folk imposing the budgeting restrictions need to be mindful of what they are actually demanding. There are practical, technical, consequences in how they are applied.

Totally fair point. Clearly absurd to distil “we want to do everything” into a meaningful portfolio of capabilities. (Although, maybe, with a big enough spreadsheet and the latest LLM … No! That way lies madness ;-) You need some focus, some way of picking out a subset of everything.

You might even use the currently-stated portfolio of capabilities, the limited remit of the products that are so enabled, ie where the org is in a sense going, as a trigger to consider a big rethink. Are we happy with what we are doing?

There are oodles of from-first-principles techniques for deciding where an org might choose go, including Wardley Maps (e.g. for identifying if hitherto high valued aspects or products are likely to become commoditised, spotting new areas of value, etc), wild-assed guesses by Hippos, services offered by any number of business consultancies, etc. Many good. Coming back to the much cited “customers are short-termist fools with no imagination” line, you will benefit from thinking about capabilities either way, whether you are still in the business of “horse-drawn carriages”, or are considering trying out the crazy new world of the automotive.

Amazon was able to spin up AWS precisely because they thought in terms of capabilities for internal projects (Bezos’s API email), settled on good, generic, useful capabilities (and lower-level systems), and then were able to sell capabilities-and-systems-as-a-service (ie AWS).

Picking apart the edgy phrase “magical central responsibility for strategic thinking” and others...

“Magical”

Ouch.

I would like to bounce this term back and suggest the term “magical” is better suited for describing the hope that things will go well when

- you think you have a strategy, but really you don’t (see Rumelt’s Good Strategy Bad Strategy)

- in practice, no-one is responsible for strategic thinking

- the ‘strategy’ is implicit, or of the “we’ll work it out as we go” variety

- impactful project decisions are routinely made in the absence of a good understanding of the strategy

“central responsibility for strategic thinking”

In this thesis, the portfolio of capabilities is an expression of the org’s strategy. If the portfolio of capabilities is not understood, agreed, and maintained at a high level, and you prioritise projects and products in its absence, you have basically guaranteed strategy-level conflicts down at project level.

It absolutely is a “central responsibility” to ensure that Product, Tech, and Business, all collaborate and agree on how a strategy is to be implemented, and hence (in this thesis) the definition of the capabilities portfolio and its consequences. Its the strategy! It has to be a CXO-level responsibility to ensure it happens effectively and is supported and enforced.

An org often already has a “magical central responsibility” for deciding which projects and products are prioritised and scheduled, called the Programme Office. Decisions are made somehow. This can only be improved with a deeper understanding of capabilities.

If there is a conflict between the ivory tower-ness of the “centrally curated capabilities” and the realities of life at the coal face, mid-project, then that certainly needs addressing. But this need not automatically be the situation. Just as possible are the benefits arising from knowing how the piece of functionality needed for any one project is part of a defined bigger picture. Synergies abound.

“an exercise in cat herding”

Sure, this is a challenge, But it is eminently doable. Referring back to the discussion under “magical”, not doing this is worse.

You want to gather a small group of competent, experienced folk, from across the business, who have the remit and time (and 'time' is possibly the most crucial aspect) to look at the bigger picture, identify useful patterns and likely useful capabilities, for ways of making the best use of the skills in product and tech, and keep an eye out for changes and new understandings. The Portfolio of Capabilities (PoC) team.

So many ways this can be tackled, including:

- Each function (Product, Tech, Business, Other) picks an appropriate rep to join the PoC team.

- The reps are rotated through the PoC team.

- It's like jury duty.

- It’s a virtual team, coming together during the time of an org’s greatest need. Badges are made. A logo, and a theme tune. A special walk.

- The PoC team hands off the paper-shuffling phase to an analyst.

- A particularly ‘analytically- and architecturally-minded’ person does the initial synthesis, and the PoC team then reviews and iterates on that.

- The PoC team generates multiple candidate portfolios, exploring different biases.

- It’s a team and a project just like all the others, so sensible project management approaches would apply.

“there is a dearth of these people”

If the issue is not lack of competence, or experience, but of coordination and approach, then a good project manager should be able to get the best out of them.

If the org truly lacks people who are capable of synthesising a list of project and product ideas into underlying capabilities, with an understanding of the bigger picture, then the org has a big problem.

Even a competent new joiner, knowing nothing of the specific org, could get up to useful speed within days. In fact, this exercise would probably be one of the better ways of onboarding them.

That would be a headline-grabbing line in a job spec: “Join us and help define the company’s strategy within your first few days!”

## Conclusion

Capability-First thinking represents a simple, subtle, but potent shift in how organisations can approach strategy and prioritisation.

First, identify a strategically relevant portfolio of versatile product capabilities, and only in that context consider how to prioritise individual products and efficiently sequence development of pieces of capabilities. This creates a solid foundation for sustainable growth and innovation.

The approach offers several key benefits:

1. Strategic alignment: Capability-First thinking ensures that every development effort contributes to the organisation’s overall strategic goals, which everyone can see. It can even form the core of that strategy.

2. Increased agility: With a strong, relevant capability base, organisations can respond more quickly to market changes and new opportunities. Teams can self-serve for quick experiments.

3. Improved collaboration: By providing a shared language and framework, Capability-First thinking bridges the gap between Product and Tech functions. Synergy rather than conflict.

4. Reduced technical debt: The focus on building well-structured, shareable capabilities from the outset, for shared+agreed reasons, minimises the accumulation of technical debt.

5. Long-term efficiency: As the capability portfolio grows, the incremental cost of developing new products decreases, leading to higher throughput and more and better quality products over time.

Organisations that embrace this approach would find themselves better equipped to navigate the complexities of today’s rapidly changing business landscape, making the most of all their in-house expertise and people.

Your future self — and your organisation — will thank you for making the shift.
